At right we see a young boy – or perhaps a dwarf? – wearing a brightly colored hat and carnival mask. From his belt hangs a large key – might it unlock the meaning of the painting? Curiously, he holds in his left hand a magnifying glass.

So, too, does the man standing next to him. Wearing a bright red robe and bautta, or tricorn hat and mask, this monumental figure peers intently through the looking glass. Is the visual emphasis on magnification that characterizes this pair an invitation for us to join their company, to look more closely?

If we look directly to the right, we see a masked boy holding a lantern. He appears to be tugging at the man’s red robe. Lantern in hand, might this figure serve to illuminate the meaning of this triangular ensemble? Or does he shed light on the larger scene unfolding in front of this masked ensemble?

Giandomenico painted other colorful sketches of Venetian life during Carnival. The Minuet, painted just three years earlier, includes a mondo nuovo, visible in the far left corner. A barker, seemingly lurking in the shadows, surveys the festive crowd. This is the first time the device appears in Giandomenico's work, albeit as a somewhat marginal reference in a Carnival scene.

Giandomenico Tiepolo, Louvre Museum, Venice, Carnival Scene (The Minuet), 1754
